The 19-millisecond ghost

Three weeks. Two on-calls. One mis-tuned connection pool. A field report on the kind of bug that shows up only in p99 dashboards — and only after you've already convinced yourself the system is fine.

PK
PK
Founder · platform · writes mostly on Sundays
28 APR 2026 14 MIN READ 184 LIKES
Share —

There is a particular kind of bug that you only meet once you have built something other people depend on. It does not appear in your local environment. It does not appear in staging. It appears in production at 03:17 on a Wednesday, on the 99th percentile of a graph that no one was looking at, and it adds nineteen milliseconds to a request that should already have returned by then.

This is the story of finding one of those bugs. It involves a vector index, a connection pool, and the kind of three-AM reasoning that is only useful in retrospect. If you skip to the end, the lesson is: your tail latency is a measurement of your system's least patient component, not its average one. But you already knew that. The interesting part is how we forgot.

§ 01 — symptomThe pager that almost wasn't

The alert fired on a synthetic check, not a real user complaint. We had wired up an SLO of p99 < 320ms on the retrieval endpoint, and for two months it sat comfortably at 280-ish, the way these things do when you've over-provisioned and underused. Then, sometime in the second week of April, the p99 line began to drift. Not spike. Drift.

By the time anyone looked, we were at 339ms. By the next morning, 358. The p50 had not moved. The p95 had moved by maybe four milliseconds. From any reasonable summary statistic, the service was healthy. From the perspective of the slowest one-in-a-hundred users, something was quietly getting worse.

What we tried first (and why it didn't help)

  • Restarted the pods. Worked for ninety minutes. Came back.
  • Increased the embedding cache TTL. Cut average latency by 12ms. Did not move p99.
  • Bisected the deploys. Every commit in the suspect range looked plausibly innocent.
  • Re-ran the benchmark suite locally. Nothing. The bug refused to leave the production blast radius.

§ 02 — instrumentationYou can't fix what you can't see

The breakthrough — if you can call something so embarrassing a breakthrough — came from finally adding span-level tracing to the part of the pipeline we had been treating as a black box. Specifically: the connection acquire step on the vector store client.

typescript · retrieval.ts// retrieval.ts — the offending hot path, with the trace we should have added on day one
import { trace } from "@/observability";
import { pool } from "@/vector-pool";

export async function retrieve(query: string, k: number = 8) {
  return trace("retrieve", async (span) => {
    const client = await trace("pool.acquire", () => pool.acquire());
    try {
      const rows = await client.query(SEARCH_SQL, [query, k]);
      span.setAttribute("rows", rows.length);
      return rows;
    } finally {
      await client.release();
    }
  });
}

The first time we saw a real waterfall, it was obvious. The pool acquire was taking 17–23ms on the slow tail, and zero everywhere else. We had a maximum pool size of 10, an average concurrency of 14, and a queue policy nobody had ever read.

"Every connection pool is a bet about how much patience the slowest user has. We had been winning that bet for so long we forgot we were placing it." — from the postmortem

§ 03 — fixThe four-line change that ate the ghost

The fix was numerically anti-climactic and architecturally embarrassing. In order:

  1. Raise maxPoolSize from 10 to 32. We had picked 10 in 2024 and never revisited it.
  2. Add a per-acquire timeout of 250ms, so backpressure is observable, not invisible.
  3. Emit a pool.queue_depth gauge into the metric pipeline that was already running.
  4. Alert when queue_depth > 4 for more than 60 seconds, escalating after 5 minutes.

Within eleven minutes of the change rolling out, p99 dropped to 264ms — lower than the baseline we had been treating as healthy. The 19ms ghost was, of course, the wait time inside the connection acquire step, surfacing only when concurrency was high enough to saturate the pool.

§ 04 — lessonWhat we changed about how we work

The technical fix took an afternoon. The cultural fix took the rest of the quarter. We made three commitments, each of which has cost us something and saved us more:

  • Trace first, optimize second. Every new dependency must arrive with at least one span around its acquire-and-release lifecycle.
  • Defaults are decisions. If a number was right two years ago, that is not a reason it is right today. We now annotate every magic constant with the date it was last reviewed.
  • If your p50 looks fine, you are looking at the wrong percentile. Tail-latency budgets are part of every SLO conversation now, not an optional bolt-on for "mature" services.

None of this is new. People have been writing about tail latency for at least a decade — Dean and Barroso's "The Tail at Scale" came out in 2013, and it remains the best four-thousand-word article in the entire field. The only thing that is novel is that it was our tail this time, not someone else's.

If you take one thing away from this note, take this: the bugs that get away from you are rarely loud. They are nineteen milliseconds long, they show up at the 99th percentile of a chart you do not look at, and they have been there longer than you think.
