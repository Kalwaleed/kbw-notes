Why we tore out our queue and let Postgres do it instead.

A field report on locks, latency, and learning to trust SKIP LOCKED — after eighteen months of chasing phantom Redis jobs across three regions.

RA Rania Al‑Mutairi Staff engineer · Riyadh · Apr 22 · 2026 · 11 min read
Share URL: /notes/why-we-tore-out-our-queue

Eighteen months ago we were a Redis shop with a side of Postgres. Today the cluster is gone, the alerting noise is gone, and the queue is just jobs — a table. This is not a manifesto. It is a journal of what changed, why, and what almost certainly will not work for you.

For the last few years there has been a quiet drift in our industry: services that used to require dedicated infrastructure are slowly being absorbed back into the database. Search. Pub/sub. Vector. Background jobs. Postgres is not the right tool for every one of these — but it is the right tool for far more of them than we admit.

§ 02 / 06What Postgres already gives you

The case for replacing a job queue with Postgres rests on three primitives, each of which has existed in the engine for over a decade:

  • SKIP LOCKED — lets a worker grab the next available row without contending with peers.
  • LISTEN/NOTIFY — gives you sub‑millisecond fan‑out without polling.
  • Transactional inserts — your job is enqueued iff the surrounding write succeeds. No eventual consistency, no orphan jobs.

Each of these alone is interesting. Together they are a queue.

The query that started it all

The whole architecture begins with one query. Read it carefully — it is the keystone:

// fetch the next available job, lock it, mark it running.
export async function claimNextJob(worker: string): Promise<Job | null> {
  const { rows } = await sql/* sql */ `
    UPDATE jobs SET
      status      = 'running',
      claimed_by  = ${worker},
      claimed_at  = now()
    WHERE id = (
      SELECT id FROM jobs
      WHERE status = 'queued' AND run_at <= now()
      ORDER BY priority DESC, run_at ASC
      LIMIT 1
      FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED
    )
    RETURNING *;
  `;
  return rows[0] ?? null;
}

That is the entire scheduler. There is no broker. There is no Lua script. The query is atomic, ordered, and fair. FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED is the magic word.

"If the only tool you keep is the database, every problem starts to look like a transaction. That is mostly a feature." — overheard, infra channel, Feb 14

§ 03 / 06The migration weekend

We migrated in three steps. Order mattered.

  1. Stand up the jobs table behind a feature flag at 0% traffic. Run shadow writes for a week to validate volume and shape.
  2. Move idempotent jobs first (email send, webhook fan‑out, image resizing). These are forgiving if they ran twice — and they did, on day three, when our deploy script outraced our migration.
  3. Move stateful jobs last, behind a kill‑switch. We left Redis up for two more weeks just in case. We didn't need it.

The weekend itself was uneventful. The most exciting moment was when the on‑call rotation realized there was nothing on the rotation anymore.

§ 04 / 06What broke (and what didn't)

Three things did break, and they're worth naming so you can sidestep them. The first was throughput at the very top of the queue: under unusual bursts (~9,800 jobs/sec, brief), workers contended on the index used by SKIP LOCKED. The fix was a partial index on (status,run_at) WHERE status='queued'. The second was a long‑running job that held its row lock through a deploy and resurfaced as a phantom on the new pod. The third was — predictably — DST.

What did not break: nearly everything else. The 95th‑percentile pickup latency dropped from 412 ms (Redis + adapter overhead) to 38 ms. P99 went from 2.1 s to 110 ms.
